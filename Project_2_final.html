Project 2¶

Scientific question¶

What is the molecular basis of naturally developed human CCR5 mutation in HIV resistance?

C-C motif chemokine receptor type 5 (CCR5)¶

CCR5 is a member of the beta chemokine receptor family composed of 7 transmembrane region (similar structure to G-protein coupled receptors). It is expressed predominantely by T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells and can bind and transduce CCL3, CCL4, and CCL5 signaling.

CCR5 also serves as an important coreceptor for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) entry into target cells. The heterotrimeric complex formed by gp120 (expressed by HIV) binding to CD4 and CCR5 (expressed on target cells) facilitates fusion of viral capsid with target cell membrane. Previous research has shown that a mutation of 32-base-pair deletion (delta-32) in the coding region of CCR5 gene which introduced a premature stop codon resulted in reduced CCR5 expression and induced HIV resistance in people carrying this mutation (Samson et al., 1996b). Mutations in other regions of CCR5 gene have been discovered, and some of which were capable of affecting CCR5 expression and structure integrity and therefore bear the potential of inducing HIV resistance in individuals carrying the mutation (Carrington et al., 1997b)(Blanpain et al., 1999b).

Hypothesis¶

If a mutation occurs on CCR5 that can influence the conformational integrity or structure of this molecule, which results in impaired CCR5 expression at cell surface or binding to gp120 expressed by HIV, then the cell carrying this mutation will be resistant to HIV infection.

Data¶

Structure of heterotrimeric complex of CD4-gp120-CCR5 is found in protein data bank (https://www.rcsb.org/) using PDB ID: 6MET (Shaik et al., 2018)https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-018-0804-9.

Structure of wild-type CCR5 was derived from the structure of CD4-gp120-CCR5 complex (6MET) using PyMol software.

Sequences of wild-type CCR5, CCR5-delta-32, and other beta chemokine receptors were found in NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gene/1234).

Sequence of CCR5 with C20S and C101A mutation was derived from wild-type CCR5 protein sequence based on information of location and amino acid involved in the mutation found in scientific papers mentioned above.

Methods¶

Structural bioinformatics was used to obtain the 3D structure of CD4-gp120-CCR5 complex to support the statement that CCR5 is an essential molecule involved facinilating HIV entrance into target cells. The structural data of CD4-gp120-CCR5 complex was obtained from PDB, and the the structure was also used to present 3D structure of wild-type CCR5 that was not bound to any inhibitors or ligands.

Homology modeling was used to obtain the 3D structures of mutated CCR5 (delta-32, C20S, and C101A), of which the structural data could not be found online with designated PDB ID. Homology modeling was performed using SWISS-MODEL (https://swissmodel.expasy.org/) with protein sequences of the mutated CCR5. This allowed visualization of overall structural difference between wild-type and mutant CCR5.

Multiple sequence alignment was used to align sequence of multiple beta chemokine receptors with CCR5. This helped to determine whether the mutation in CCR5 examined occured in conserved region of the beta chemokine receptor family. Protein sequences used for multiple sequence alignment were found on NCBI. Homology modeling was performed using MAFFT online server (https://mafft.cbrc.jp/alignment/server/).

3D protein measurement was used to quantify difference in structures between the wild-type CCR5 and the mutated versions. Protein sequences were directly extracted from PDB files obtained in homology modeling.

Sequence logos was used to visualize the level of conservation at each amino acid position to determine if the mutations examined occured at conserved region of beta chemokine receptor family. The aligned protein sequences used for sequence logos were obtained from multiple sequence alignment.

    # visualize the struture in file and assign the visualized structure to local variable view
    view = nv.show_structure_file(file)
    # clear all presentation format of the structure
    view.clear()
    # present the structure in assigned format, selected portion, and color
    view.add_representation(struc_format, selection=structure_sele,color=structure_color)
    # show structure
    return view

# source code: https://towardsdatascience.com/visualizing-and-analyzing-proteins-in-python-bd99521ccd
# define a function protein_measurement to perform 3D protein measurement of proteins in pdb files
# parameter protein is the name of the protein to be analyzed
# parameter pdb_file is file to be read in and analyzed
# both parameters are local variables
def protein_measurement(protein, pdb_file):
    # extract protein sequence from the pdb file
    for record in SeqIO.parse(pdb_file, "pdb-atom"):
        # assign sequence to local variable sequence
        sequence=record.seq
    # uncomment below for code check
    # print(sequence)
    
    # convert sequence to the correct type for analysis and assign to local variable analyzed_seq
    analyzed_seq = ProteinAnalysis(str(sequence))
    # uncomment below for code check
    # print(type(analyzed_seq))
    
    # create an emtpy dictionary to store protein analysis result and assign the dictionary to local variable seq_analysis
    seq_analysis = {}
    # perform analysis
    # assign the name of analyzed protein as value to key sequence name
    seq_analysis['Sequence name'] = protein
    # assign the sequence of analyzed protein as value to key sequence
    seq_analysis['Sequence'] = str(sequence)
    # assign the length of sequence of analyzed protein as value to key sequence length
    seq_analysis['Sequence length'] = len(sequence)
    # assign the molecular weight of analyzed protein as value to key molecular weight
    seq_analysis['Molecular weight'] = analyzed_seq.molecular_weight()
    # assign the hydrophobicity level of analyzed protei as value to key GRAVY
    seq_analysis['GRAVY'] = analyzed_seq.gravy()
    # assign the secondary structure of analyzed protein (a tuple of helix, turn, and sheet) as value to key secondary structure
    seq_analysis['Secondary structure'] = analyzed_seq.secondary_structure_fraction()
    # uncomment below for code check
    # print(seq_info)
    
    # convert the dictionary into a pandas dataframe and assign dataframe to local variable seq_df
    seq_df=pd.DataFrame.from_dict(seq_analysis, orient='index')
    return seq_df

Result analysis¶

Structure of CCR5 with C20S or C101A mutation does not show great variation from the wildtype. Structure of CCR5 with delta-32 mutation shows that this mutation will cause this protein to lose its conformational integrety. Since CCR5 is required for HIV entry into the cell by forming CD4-gp120-CCR5 complex, delta-32 mutation is expected to hinder the function of CCR5 as coreceptor for HIV entrance. This could result in the development of HIV resistance, which is proven by research demonstrating that people with CCR5-delta-32 allele are resitant to HIV infection. Although C20S and C101A mutation do not seeme to influence the overall secondary structure of CCR5 based on result from homology modeling, point mutation in cystine could result in a loss of disulfide bond, which could also influence either the structure or the conformational integrity of the protein. Therefore, these two mutation could potentialy hinder the ability of CCR5 serving as coreceptor of HIV entry into host cells. This is also proven by research demonstrating that cells carrying genes with C20S mutation or C101A mutation have reduced expression level of CCR5 at cell surface, which in turn reduced HIV infection. Therefore, it is likely that a mutation in CCR5 which could alter the structure of the protein and reduce its expression at cells surface could cause the development of HIV resistance. Interestingly, the two point mutations analyzed, C20S and C101A, both occured in highly conserved region throughout beta-chemokine receptor. This could indicate that the mutations could potentialy have larger impact on CCR5 function than expected from the strucutural difference demonstrated by homology modeling and 3D protein measurement.
